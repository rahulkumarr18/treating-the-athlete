Simply put: a class where you learn to be an athlete's doctor.

When an athlete gets hurt, a lot happens fast. Someone has to check them on the field, decide if it's serious, put a dislocated joint back, spot a concussion, and know when it needs surgery. That's sports medicine, and medical school barely touches it.

Treating the Athlete is a short, optional class at UMass Chan that fills the gap. Over seven evenings, sports-medicine and orthopedic doctors walk you through how they actually take care of athletes. Some of the nights are hands-on, so you get to try the skills yourself. No experience needed, no exams, and it's open to any UMass Chan medical student.

What you'll learn

Six things every athlete's doctor should know

Each evening takes on one piece of caring for athletes, in plain terms, with real cases.

Cover the game

Be the doctor on the sideline: what to watch for, how to run an emergency plan, and what's in the medical bag.

Reset a joint

Recognize a dislocated shoulder or joint and learn how doctors safely pop it back into place on the field.

Know when it's surgery

See how orthopedic surgeons decide when an injury, like an unstable shoulder, needs an operation and when it doesn't.

Handle concussions

Learn the simple tools doctors use to check for a head injury and decide when it's safe to return to play.

Train and fuel athletes

The real evidence behind strength training, conditioning, nutrition, and supplements, so you can give athletes honest advice.

Practice with your hands

Two in-person nights: try point-of-care ultrasound and hands-on techniques yourself, with the faculty guiding you.
